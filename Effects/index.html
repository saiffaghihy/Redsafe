Effects

Drug and alcohol abuse in high school does not just affect the person using. It creates ripples that spread through their academics, relationships, mental health, and future. When people talk about addiction, they often picture the extreme, but the truth is, most of the damage starts small and silently. It shows up in everyday life, in the little things, and before you know it, those effects begin to stack up.

One of the first places substance use shows up is academic performance. It becomes harder to focus, to stay motivated, to keep up. Students who start drinking or using drugs often experience memory issues, poor time management, and missing assignments. That’s not because they suddenly stop caring, it’s because the brain itself changes. As we learned in class, substances like alcohol and marijuana affect the hippocampus, which is responsible for learning and memory. So when students use, especially consistently, they physically lose the ability to retain what they learn in class. Even missing just a few days of concentration can have long-term academic consequences. Students fall behind, grades drop, and stress increases, which can lead to even more use. It becomes a cycle.

The mental health effects are even more serious. Many students start using substances because they feel anxious, stressed, sad, or overwhelmed. But instead of solving the issue, substances actually make it worse. Alcohol is a depressant, meaning it lowers serotonin levels and increases feelings of sadness or numbness. Drugs that give a high, like nicotine or THC, often leave students feeling worse during the comedown. Over time, this leads to increased anxiety, depression, and even suicidal thoughts. In class, we talked about how chronic substance use disrupts the brain’s dopamine system, so the things that used to bring natural joy, like sports, music, or friendships, no longer feel rewarding. This leaves students feeling hollow, and they often don’t know why.

Another major effect is relationship damage. When someone starts using, they begin to change. They may lie to friends and family, hide what they’re doing, isolate themselves, or become emotionally distant. Trust breaks down quickly. Friendships start to fade. Family members begin to worry or get angry. The person struggling may not even realize how much they’ve changed until it’s too late. In class, we talked about how addiction can cause “denial-based behavior,” like minimizing the problem, shifting blame, or avoiding conversations. These defense mechanisms often push people away, even when they’re trying to help. And when relationships fall apart, the person using often feels more alone, which pushes them further into the addiction cycle.

Then there’s physical health, which is often overlooked at first. Vaping damages lung tissue, especially in teenagers whose lungs are still developing. Alcohol can affect the liver, sleep cycles, and heart function. Pills, weed, and other drugs can affect appetite, body weight, energy levels, and immune function. Many students using substances report chronic fatigue, headaches, nausea, or unexplained mood swings. What may start as a “once in a while” thing can quietly wear down the body over time. And once the physical effects begin, the mental toll gets even worse.

One of the most dangerous long-term effects is addiction itself. Not every student who tries something becomes addicted, but many do. In class, we learned that the earlier someone starts using, the higher the chance they develop dependency later in life. The brain builds tolerance, meaning the same amount of a substance no longer has the same effect. That leads to increased use, stronger substances, and more risk. Eventually, the person doesn’t use to feel good anymore, they use just to feel normal. That’s when quitting becomes incredibly difficult. Withdrawal symptoms like anxiety, depression, irritability, or even physical pain kick in, and most students don’t know how to get out of it alone.

There are also legal and safety consequences. Getting caught with substances at school or outside of it can lead to suspension, expulsion, or even a criminal record. This can affect college applications, job opportunities, and future goals. Some students end up in unsafe situations while under the influence, like getting into a car with a drunk driver, having non-consensual encounters, or putting themselves in risky environments they wouldn’t normally be in. The truth is, most students don’t intend for these things to happen, but substances cloud judgment, and one bad decision can change everything.

Finally, there is the effect on identity. Substances often blur the line between who someone was and who they become. Many students report feeling like they don’t recognize themselves anymore. They become disconnected from the things they used to care about, like sports, art, music, or their own goals. They begin to lose their sense of direction. And for high school students, who are still figuring out who they are, that kind of identity loss can be incredibly hard to recover from.

The effects of substance abuse are real, even if they don’t always show on the outside. They can be silent, slow, and hidden. But they shape lives in ways that students don’t always realize until the damage has been done. This is why early education, open conversation, and support matter. The earlier we intervene, the more students we protect from those long-term consequences.
